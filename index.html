MY DECAGON STORY

I got wind of this training on a WhatsApp group set up for volunteer, experienced and entry-level professionals which creates awareness of available opportunities as they arise. I actually only found out about Decagon's SQ004 recruitment after the original assessment date was cancelled. I was a whisker away from missing out but I hurried to register and completed the requisite Free Code Camp html, css and javascript introductory courses in preparation for that weekend's online assessment. Flash back to two months earlier, when a friend of mine shared a flyer advertising a 2-day training in web design. There, I received basic html and css coaching. This was my first real attempt at writing code. I was thus able to focus on the FCC javascript course content and learn as much as I could in the limited time available. I was first introduced to programming as an undergraduate and taught to cram principles in writing V-Basic code. To put it mildly, I hated it. That bad experience left me with the impression that I had no future writing code or developing software.

For several years I attempted to try my hands on learning to code only to backed off immediately it appeared to get a little confusing. I never did get far beyond "HELLO WORLD". In researching an Engineering Design masters degree programme I was interested in, I found I had developed interest in Robotics. Further digging brought to my knowledge that in order to build a career in Robotics, Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning I needed to learn to write code using Python programming language. This discovery reignited my interest in acquiring coding skills. That 2-day training and my study period with Decagon thus far have made me realise that I possess the aptitude to excel as a Software Engineer.

The online assessment was a breeze and I was sure I'd passed but after the physical interview at the Ikoyi office, I again began to doubt my powers. I knew the algorithm used to test us was easy because I had only 7 minutes to solve it therefore, the fact that I failed to muster an answer was discouraging. I could explain my attempted solution though, and I believe this was why I was able to scrape my way into the BootCamp. I believe you can already guess that prior to getting into the BootCamp, I hadn't managed to solve any of the Home Study Lab questions and two days into the BootCamp, I still hadn't solved any. I was on the verge of deciding to drop out because I feared that anytime now, I would receive a dreaded email asking me to stay home and never return. I chose to brave it out. I chose to stay. On the third day of BootCamp, I read an article which changed everything for me.


Thinking Like A Programmer

by Victor Nnaji


I'd recommend that article to any beginner who is experiencing difficulty learning to proffer solutions to algorithms. My knowledge was limited, yes, but my biggest mistake was fear. I failed to understand that problems are essentially solvable and breaking down a seemingly large problem into smaller solution steps meant I could attack even the most complex algorithms. Indeed, I still face some problems which I cannot yet solve but I'll keep chipping away and improving my level of understanding knowing that someday, I will overcome those as well. I scaled the three week probation period by solving katas on Codewars and learning everyday. I am certainly better now than I was. I'm a DECADEV!!!
